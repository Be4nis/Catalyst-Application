Bonding & VSPER Theory

Learning About VSEPR Theory

Valence Shell Electron Theory was a topic I found myself struggling with. When I was initially exposed to this topic I felt very confused. I didn't know how to determine the shape of different chemical compounds. After a lot of practice, questions and memorizing I now know that a VSEPR diagram is used to explain the 3D geometry of molecules and is kind of like a 3D lewis dot diagram. To practice VSEPR theory, I would draw a compound using the Lewis Dot Diagram and then draw the same compound using a VSEPR diagram. I finally understood that the VSEPR model is an extremely useful tool we to predict the structure of molecules and polyatomic ions with a non-metal as the middle atom. I have certainly not mastered VSEPR theory but I am proud of myself for how far I have come since first seeing a VSEPR diagram.

Intermolecular Forces/Van Der Waals Forces

Intermolecular forces weren't a hard concept for me to understand. I knew that every covalent bond has forces keeping it together. London Dispersion Forces were easy to understand, every molecule has this force and the polarizability, the stronger the LDFs. Hydrogen bonding was also easy to identify, if a hydrogen molecule is connected with nitrogen, oxygen or flourine then it is a hydrogen bond. Dipole-Dipole forces happen when two polar molecules interact and ion-dipole forces happen when an ion and a dipole (polar molecule) interact. To do these questions I just had to know that LDF's weakest, followed by dipole-dipole, hydrogen bonding and then ion-dipole which is the strongest. Overall I found intermolecular forces pretty simple and easy to understand.
